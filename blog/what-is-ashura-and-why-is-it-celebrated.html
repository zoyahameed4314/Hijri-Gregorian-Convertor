What is Ashura and why is it celebrated?

Ashura is the tenth day of the Islamic month Muharram. People often ask what the day of Ashura is. It is a day of massive historical and religious importance. Sunni Muslims celebrate it as a day of divine rescue. They fast to remember how God saved Prophet Musa. God saved him and his followers from the cruel Pharaoh. This happened through the miraculous parting the Red Sea. Shia Muslims observe the exact same day very differently. They spend this day in intense, heartfelt mourning. They remember the tragic death of Imam Hussain ibn Ali. He was the beloved grandson of the Prophet Muhammad (PBUH). He was brutally killed at the Battle of Karbala. This historic battle took place in the year 61 AH. This corresponds exactly to 680 CE in the Gregorian calendar (you can easily verify this using our Bidirectional Date Converter). Both events define why Ashura is widely observed today. It remains a unique day of both gratitude and profound grief.

The Meaning of the Word Ashura

The word Ashura comes directly from ancient Arabic origins. It is derived from the Arabic word for ten. Therefore, it simply translates to the tenth day. It always falls in Muharram, a highly sacred month. The Islamic Lunar Calendar features twelve distinct lunar months. Muharram serves as the very first month of this calendar (to learn more about how this year begins, read What Happened on the First Day of Muharram?). The actual name Muharram translates to forbidden or highly sacred. Warfare and violence were strictly banned during this holy time. Even pre-Islamic Arab tribes respected this ancient rule of peace. This specific context makes the tragedy of Karbala even worse. Blood was violently shed during a month meant for ultimate peace. Muslims worldwide pay very close attention to this specific month. They track the moon to calculate the date of Ashura. It marks the beginning of the spiritual New Year. It sets a solemn tone for the months ahead.

Prophet Musa and the Parting of the Red Sea

The earliest history of Ashura connects deeply to Prophet Musa. He is also widely known as Moses in the West. Prophet Musa faced a terrifying enemy in ancient Egypt. The Pharaoh ruled the land with absolute, unmatched cruelty. He enslaved the Israelites and caused them immense physical suffering. He even ordered the death of innocent newborn boys. Prophet Musa bravely led his people out of this brutal slavery. They ran toward the sea to escape the royal army. Pharaoh and his massive military forces chased them aggressively. The situation looked completely hopeless for the fleeing Israelites. They were trapped between the deep water and the army. Panic quickly spread among the terrified men and women. Then, a famous and powerful divine miracle suddenly occurred. God commanded Prophet Musa to strike the ocean water. He used his wooden staff to hit the crashing waves. This resulted in the magnificent parting the Red Sea. The Israelites walked safely across the dry ocean floor. Pharaoh and his heavily armed troops tried to follow them. They rushed blindly into the middle of the parted sea. The massive walls of water suddenly crashed down upon them. The entire Egyptian army drowned in the deep, dark sea. Prophet Musa was overwhelmed with deep, genuine spiritual gratitude. He began fasting on the day to thank God. His followers joined him in this holy act of worship.

Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) and the Tradition of Fasting

Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) deeply respected this ancient prophetic tradition. He migrated from the city of Mecca to Medina. There, he noticed the local Jewish community fasting. He respectfully asked them why they fasted on this day. They explained they were honoring Prophet Musa and the Exodus. Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) felt a very close spiritual connection to Prophet Musa. He declared that Muslims also had a strong right to Musa. He told his eager followers to adopt this specific fast. Fasting (Sawm) on this day became a vital Islamic tradition. Sunni Muslims continue this exact practice to this modern day. They fast to show immense gratitude for God's divine mercy. Many also fast on the ninth day of Muharram. Adding a day makes the Islamic fast slightly unique. This adds extra spiritual merit to the act of worship. The fast is strictly optional but highly recommended by scholars. It provides a quiet day of personal reflection and prayer. It effectively wipes away minor sins from the previous calendar year.

Imam Hussain and the Battle of Karbala

Decades later, the historic meaning of Ashura changed forever. The expanding Islamic empire faced a severe leadership crisis. A corrupt and wealthy ruler named Yazid ibn Muawiyah seized power. He demanded absolute public loyalty from all prominent Muslim leaders. Imam Hussain ibn Ali completely refused to support Yazid. Hussain ibn Ali was the noble grandson of the Prophet (PBUH). He simply could not endorse a deeply corrupt, cruel tyrant. He viewed Yazid as a direct threat to Islamic values. Imam Hussain left his home city to avoid bloody conflict. He traveled toward Iraq with his family and closest friends. Yazid's massive, heavily armed army intercepted them in the desert. This harsh, unforgiving desert area was known as Karbala. The date was the early days of the month Muharram. The Islamic year was exactly 61 AH. Yazid's ruthless army trapped Hussain's small, highly devoted caravan. The army intentionally blocked their access to the Euphrates River. Men, women, and young children suffered extreme, painful thirst. They endured this terrible deprivation for three long days. The heat of the Iraqi desert was absolutely brutal.

The Tenth Day at Karbala

The tenth day of the month finally arrived. This was the fateful Day of Ashura. Imam Hussain had only about seventy-two loyal supporters remaining. They faced an opposing military force of thousands of soldiers. The famous Battle of Karbala began early in the morning. Hussain's companions fought with incredible bravery, skill, and honor. One by one, they were brutally killed in the sand. Even Hussain's infant baby son was killed by an arrow. By the late afternoon, Imam Hussain stood entirely alone. He fought valiantly despite his incredibly heavy wounds and thirst. Eventually, he fell to the ground and was tragically killed. The enemy army violently looted his camp and burned tents. They captured the grieving women and the terrified children. This horrific event shocked the entire early Muslim world. The beloved grandson of the Prophet was slaughtered on Ashura. The spiritual landscape of this day was permanently altered forever.

How Do Shia Muslims Observe Ashura?

Shia Muslims view Ashura primarily through the lens of Karbala. It is the absolute peak of their annual religious mourning. They do not focus heavily on celebrating the Exodus story. Instead, they focus entirely on the tragic sacrifice of Hussain. They hold large, emotional community gatherings called Majlis. Here, they listen to powerful speeches about the historic battle. Talented poets recite deeply moving elegies about the fallen heroes. People weep openly to show their profound, unhidden grief. This intense sorrow is a sign of immense spiritual love. Many Shia Muslims participate in massive public mourning processions. They wear simple black clothing to symbolize their deep sadness. Some gently beat their chests in traditional communal mourning rituals. This physical act is called Latmiya or Matam in different cultures. They distribute free food and cold water to passing strangers. This specifically honors the agonizing thirst Hussain suffered at Karbala. Ashura is a day of immense, undeniable emotional weight. It is a harsh reminder of the heavy cost of justice.

The Role of Lady Zainab

The profound story of Ashura does not end on the battlefield. Imam Hussain's brave sister played a massive historical role. Her name was Lady Zainab. She survived the brutal battle and became a captive prisoner. She was forced to march in chains to Yazid's palace. Despite her intense grief, she delivered incredibly powerful, fearless speeches. She publicly exposed Yazid's extreme cruelty to the entire empire. Her loud, confident voice preserved the true story of the battle. Without her, the tragedy of Karbala might have been entirely forgotten. Shia Muslims honor her immense strength during every Ashura gathering. She represents the ultimate power of speaking truth to tyranny.

A Shared Day of Spiritual Reflection

Ashura clearly holds two very different emotional realities for Muslims. One reality is a joyful celebration of divine rescue. The other reality is a tearful memorial of supreme sacrifice. Yet, both of these historic stories share a powerful core theme. They are both primarily about standing up against cruel tyranny. Prophet Musa bravely stood up to the arrogant, powerful Pharaoh. Imam Hussain bravely stood up to the corrupt, violent Yazid. Both amazing men risked absolutely everything for justice and faith. They both completely refused to bow down to dark oppression. This shared theme makes Ashura a universal lesson for humanity. It actively teaches people to resist injustice in every single era. Both Sunni and Shia Muslims respect this vital underlying message together. The day reminds everyone that true faith requires great courage.

Global Cultural Practices

Ashura is observed differently across various parts of the world. In Iraq, millions of devoted pilgrims travel to the city Karbala. They visit the magnificent shrine of Imam Hussain in person. In South Asia, Muslims prepare special food to share widely. They create sweet, cold drinks to hand out in the streets. This charity provides comfort in the name of the martyrs. In parts of North Africa, some cultures treat it like a holiday. They buy new clothes and cook generous family meals. The diversity of these cultural practices highlights the day's vast impact.

How to Calculate the Date of Ashura

Many religious beginners wonder how to calculate the date of Ashura. The exact date shifts every single year on the Gregorian calendar. This happens because the Islamic Lunar Calendar is much shorter. It relies entirely on the changing phases of the moon. A standard lunar year has only about 354 days total. This is roughly eleven days shorter than a standard solar year. Therefore, the month of Muharram begins on a different day annually. Islamic scholars must physically sight the new moon to begin Muharram. Once the tiny new moon is seen, the counting begins. Ashura always falls exactly on the tenth day. Local community mosques typically announce the precise date in advance. This helps people prepare for their specific fasting or mourning rituals.

The Spiritual Power of Fasting

Fasting on the day is a highly respected spiritual practice. It involves abstaining completely from food and water until sunset. It is not merely a physical exercise in simple starvation. It is an exercise in building strong personal self-control. Fasting helps purify the human heart and clear the busy mind. It builds deep empathy for those who are currently starving globally. The Prophet highly praised fasting during the entire month of Muharram. He ranked it right below the mandatory fasting of Ramadan. This clearly highlights the massive spiritual weight of the entire month. It offers a beautiful chance for deep spiritual renewal.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

1. What is forbidden during Ashura?

Warfare and violence are strictly forbidden during the sacred month Muharram. Ashura is meant to be a completely peaceful, deeply spiritual day. Many Muslims avoid planning weddings or major celebrations on Ashura. They do this out of deep respect for the Karbala tragedy. Eating and drinking are forbidden during daylight if one fasts.

2. Was Jesus born on Ashura?

No reliable historical Islamic texts state Jesus was born then. Some weak cultural myths claim many past prophets were born then. However, authentic Islamic theology does not support this specific claim. Jesus is a highly revered, beloved prophet in the Islamic faith. His miraculous birth is not officially linked to the tenth day.

3. What do Muslims do on the Day of Ashura?

Practices heavily depend on a person's specific religious sect and culture. Sunni Muslims typically fast, pray, and read the Quran quietly. Shia Muslims attend large public mourning gatherings and cry for Hussain ibn Ali. Many people from both groups generously give food to the poor. It is a busy day of intense spiritual focus everywhere.

4. Do Jews observe Ashura?

Jews do not observe Ashura under that specific Arabic name. However, the underlying history shares deep roots with Jewish traditions. The major Jewish holiday of Yom Kippur often falls near Ashura. Yom Kippur involves fasting and asking God for deep forgiveness. Both historic days honor Prophet Musa and the Exodus from Egypt.
